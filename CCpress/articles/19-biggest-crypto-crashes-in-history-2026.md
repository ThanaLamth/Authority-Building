## What were the biggest crypto crashes in history?

The biggest crypto crashes in history include the 2011 Mt. Gox flash crash, the 2013 China- and overheat-driven collapses, the 2018 post-bubble unwind, the March 2020 COVID liquidity shock, the 2022 FTX collapse, and the more recent 2025-2026 macro and policy-driven selloffs. The exact ranking changes depending on whether you use absolute dollars, percentage drops, or market-wide contagion. For readers, the more useful lens is which crashes changed behavior for the longest time.

## The full list

### 1. The 2011 Mt. Gox flash crash

This crash remains foundational because it proved early that exchange weakness could create absurd price dislocation and destroy confidence quickly.

### 2. The April 2013 boom-and-bust reversal

2013 taught the market that vertical retail-driven rallies can unwind just as fast when infrastructure is weak and sentiment outruns reality.

### 3. The December 2013 China crackdown crash

Policy shocks entered the crypto playbook early. The China-driven selloff showed that state action could trigger fast repricing even in a market that marketed itself as borderless.

### 4. The 2018 post-ATH bear market

The 2018 unwind matters because it was the industry’s great “narrative reset.” Countless weak projects disappeared, and the market learned again that hype-heavy cycles can take years to repair.

### 5. The March 2020 COVID liquidity crash

This crash mattered because it linked crypto more visibly to global liquidity conditions. In stress, the asset class did not behave like a magical hedge. It behaved like a risk asset under pressure.

### 6. The May 2021 deleveraging and policy shock

This period remains important because it mixed Tesla- and ESG-related narrative damage with Chinese mining and trading restrictions, producing a sharp repricing of confidence.

### 7. The 2022 Terra and contagion unwind

Terra belongs on the list because it turned structural weakness in one ecosystem into a wider market trust crisis.

### 8. The November 2022 FTX collapse

FTX deserves a separate slot because it was not just another bad market month. It was a failure of institutional-looking trust that accelerated one of the harshest repricings in modern crypto. Readers who want that trust-failure angle in more detail should continue with [The Biggest Crypto Exchange Collapses](/biggest-crypto-exchange-collapses).

### 9. The 2025 tariff and macro shock

Decrypt’s crash retrospective points to the tariff-shock era as another reminder that crypto remains exposed to wider macro policy stress.

### 10. The February 2026 wipeout

CoinDesk’s February 1, 2026 report on a roughly $19 billion overnight crypto wipeout shows that violent re-pricing remains very much alive. Even in a more mature market, reflexive liquidation and exchange-led panic can still hit fast.

## The recurring pattern behind every major crash

The triggers change, but the structure is familiar:

- leverage builds
- confidence hardens into certainty
- a catalyst breaks the story
- liquidity vanishes faster than expected

That is why crypto crash history is still useful. It reminds readers that every cycle creates a new excuse for a very old market behavior.

## What this tells us about crypto in 2026

Crypto is maturing, but not becoming calm. The asset class still responds violently to policy shocks, custody shocks, credit shocks, and macro stress. The main difference is that each crash now teaches the market something more specific:

- trust matters more than slogans
- liquidity can be fake until tested
- correlation returns during panic
- institutional branding does not remove reflexive risk

What stood out in the historical review was not that every crash was unique. It was that the market keeps finding new stories to express the same old weaknesses. That is useful for readers because it turns history into pattern recognition rather than trivia.

## FAQ

### Which crash mattered most historically?

Mt. Gox mattered most for custody trust. FTX mattered most for institutional-looking credibility. The 2018 and 2022 crashes mattered most for cycle-wide narrative destruction.

### Is crypto less crash-prone now?

Not really. It may be broader and more liquid in places, but it still reprices violently when leverage and confidence break together.

### Why include 2025 and 2026 in a history list?

Because crash history should stay current. Newer crashes show how much has changed and how much has not.
