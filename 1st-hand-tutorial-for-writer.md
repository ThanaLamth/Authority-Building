# 1st hand tutorial for writer

This tutorial is a practical writing formula for making list, comparison, and review articles feel first-hand, credible, and editorial instead of generic or promotional.

## Core principle

Do not pretend you tested more than you actually tested.

If you only reviewed a public product flow, say that clearly.

If you completed a real setup or usage test, describe what actually happened.

The writing should feel like:

- direct
- observed
- balanced
- useful
- specific

## Core writing style

The voice should sound like an editor with judgment, not a marketer and not an encyclopedia.

Use sentences like:

- `The important thing is not X. The important thing is Y.`
- `What stood out immediately was...`
- `This is a strength if..., but a weakness if...`
- `For this type of reader, that tradeoff matters more than...`
- `In practice, the better choice depends on...`

Avoid:

- hype language
- empty praise
- generic "best ever" statements
- emotion that does not come from real observation

## Intro formula

The intro should be two short paragraphs.

Paragraph 1:

- name the real problem
- speak to the user's practical decision

Paragraph 2:

- explain how this article evaluates the options
- show that the piece has editorial judgment

Example:

`If you are choosing an NFT minting platform, the real problem is usually not feature count. The real problem is which tool gets you from idea to launch without creating the wrong kind of friction.`

`That is why this article does not rank platforms by feature list alone. We are looking at them through the lens of setup friction, control, onboarding, and long-term fit for different types of teams.`

## Trust block formula

Put a short trust block near the top of the article.

It should say:

- what you reviewed
- when you reviewed it
- what was checked directly
- what still needs deeper verification

Example:

> Why you can trust this guide
>
> This article is based on live product pages and current documentation reviewed in July 2026. Where a claim still depends on a logged-in workflow, live pricing, or a full end-to-end test, we mark it for final verification before publication.

## First-hand section formula

Use a real section inside the body, not a note to editors.

Good H2 examples:

- `What we checked ourselves before ranking these tools`
- `Our direct read after reviewing the live product flows`
- `What changed once we looked at the actual setup experience`

The section should do four things:

1. Explain what you directly reviewed.
2. Explain the limit of that review.
3. Show visual evidence.
4. State what stood out.

Example:

`To write this comparison, we reviewed the live public product surfaces of the shortlisted platforms and compared how each one frames the launch workflow. We did that so the article would not depend only on feature lists and third-party roundups.`

`That direct review does not replace a full end-to-end usage test. But it does make one thing clear very quickly: some platforms are optimizing for creator control, some for onboarding, and some for infrastructure flexibility.`

## First-person formula

Use first person only when it reflects a real action.

Safe phrases:

- `we reviewed`
- `we noticed`
- `our first impression was`
- `from the public flow we reviewed`
- `based on what we could verify directly`
- `what stood out immediately was`

If the team only reviewed public pages, say:

- `From the public product surface we reviewed...`
- `Based on the current live flow...`
- `What we could verify directly from the public experience was...`

If the team ran a real test, say:

- `In our test run...`
- `During setup...`
- `When we completed the workflow...`

Never use:

- `we used this for months` unless true
- `we tested everything` unless true
- `we found this perfect` unless you can defend it

## What stood out formula

This is the sentence pattern that makes the article feel observed.

Examples:

- `What stood out immediately was not the number of features. It was the posture of the product.`
- `The clearest difference was not capability. It was where the platform puts friction in the workflow.`
- `Even before a logged-in test, the product already signals whether it is built for creators, brand teams, or developers.`

This section should always end in judgment.

Example:

`That makes this platform stronger for teams that need expansion and control, but weaker for teams that only want the shortest path to launch.`

## Balanced evaluation formula

Every strong paragraph should carry both upside and downside.

Pattern:

1. state the strength
2. explain why it matters
3. state the weakness
4. explain who should avoid it

Example:

`Thirdweb is strong because it reduces the gap between a fast launch and a more expandable stack later. But that same flexibility creates more decisions early in the process, which can feel like overhead if your only goal is a lightweight creator drop.`

## Tool review formula

Use the same structure for every tool or product.

```md
### Tool Name

X is a strong choice for [use case]. From the public flow we reviewed, it immediately felt more like [type of product] than [other type]. That is a strength if you need [benefit], but it can become a weakness if your priority is [other need].

Best for:
- ...
- ...

Tradeoffs:
- ...
- ...
- ...
```

## Visual evidence formula

The screenshots should not sit in the article silently. Mention them in the prose.

Examples:

- `The screenshots above show why this matters: the platform presents itself as infrastructure first, not as a lightweight creator wizard.`
- `That visual difference is not cosmetic. It signals which kind of user the product expects.`
- `Even before a logged-in test, the interface already tells you whether the product is optimized for control, speed, or mainstream onboarding.`

## Caption formula

Captions should sound editorial and SEO-clean.

Examples:

- `Thirdweb homepage captured during our July 2026 review of NFT minting platforms.`
- `Crossmint product surface reviewed as part of our comparison of NFT minting tools.`
- `Zora creator publishing homepage captured during our July 2026 platform review.`

Avoid captions that mention internal tools, repos, or workflow details irrelevant to readers.

## Quantitative writing formula

When you have real data, use it.

Examples:

- setup time
- time to first successful action
- number of steps before checkout
- measured speed or outcome

When you do not have real data yet, be honest.

Example:

`At this stage, we are comfortable describing the workflow difference qualitatively, but not yet assigning a hard time-to-launch number until the live test is complete.`

## Anti-PR rule

Every compliment must have a counterweight.

Examples:

- `This is powerful, but...`
- `This is cleaner, but...`
- `This feels easier, but...`
- `This is the better option for X, not for everyone.`

If a section only praises, it will read like sponsored copy.

## Closing formula

The ending should help a reader choose.

Pattern:

- choose A if...
- choose B if...
- choose C if...

Example:

`Choose Thirdweb if you need the best overall balance between control and long-term flexibility. Choose Crossmint if mainstream onboarding is the hardest problem in your launch. Choose Zora if your release is really a creator-publishing play rather than a generic sales workflow.`

## Fast checklist for writers

- Did we say what we directly checked?
- Did we avoid overstating the depth of our testing?
- Did we use first person only where it is true?
- Did we include at least one observed judgment, not just a feature summary?
- Did we show both strength and weakness?
- Did we use captions that sound editorial, not technical?
- Did we avoid hype?
- Did we make the final recommendation specific?
