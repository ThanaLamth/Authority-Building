## Why global liquidity matters for Bitcoin

Bitcoin often responds to changes in the availability of capital, risk appetite, and central bank balance-sheet expansion. The relationship is not mechanical. Bitcoin is not a leveraged M2 chart. But liquidity conditions shape the background environment in which BTC trades, and understanding whether that environment is tightening or loosening is a legitimate part of macro market analysis.

The useful question is not whether BTC always follows liquidity. It is: when does the relationship tighten, when does it break, and what explains the gap? Those are answerable questions when the chart methodology is transparent. They are not answerable when the chart is a social-media screenshot with unlabeled axes and an unlabeled liquidity series.

## What global liquidity means in each chart

The phrase global liquidity is used loosely across the charts on this list. Each uses a different underlying proxy. Understanding which proxy is being used is the first step in evaluating whether the chart is informative.

**M2 money supply** is the broadest widely-tracked measure of the money supply in a single country. The most common variant in Bitcoin analysis uses a composite of U.S., EU, Japan, and China M2 converted to USD at current exchange rates. FRED provides the individual national series. MacroMicro aggregates them into a composite view.

**Central bank balance sheets** measure total assets held by major central banks, which expand when QE programs purchase assets and contract when those assets are sold or mature. CrossBorder Capital uses a formal balance-sheet composite rather than M2 alone.

**Dollar-liquidity composites** adjust global liquidity for the strength of the U.S. dollar, because a stronger dollar tightens financial conditions globally even if raw M2 is flat. Some TradingView overlays use DXY-adjusted liquidity rather than raw M2.

**Stablecoin supply** is a crypto-native liquidity proxy. Growing stablecoin supply means dry powder is accumulating inside the crypto ecosystem. It is not a substitute for global macro liquidity but it is a useful parallel signal. Artemis and Dune provide this data directly.

Knowing which proxy a chart uses changes how it should be interpreted. A chart using DXY-adjusted global liquidity will behave differently from a raw M2 chart during periods of dollar strength.

## How to build the most common BTC-versus-liquidity overlay on TradingView

The most reproducible BTC-versus-liquidity overlay uses FRED series pulled into TradingView. These are the steps:

1. Open TradingView and set the primary chart to BTCUSD on a weekly timeframe.
2. Click Add indicator and select Compare or add symbol.
3. Add `FRED:M2SL` for U.S. M2 money supply. This is the St. Louis Fed Money Stock Measures series.
4. To add a rough global M2 composite, also add `FRED:JPNASSETS` (Bank of Japan total assets) and `FRED:ECBASSETS` (ECB total assets) as additional overlays.
5. Normalize each series to percentage change from a common start date to make the overlay readable.
6. Alternatively, search for community indicators using keywords global liquidity or M2 BTC in the TradingView indicator library. Several community scripts aggregate these series automatically.

The limitation of step 6 is that community scripts have varying methodology quality. Always check which series a community indicator is pulling before citing it in published analysis. A script that says global liquidity but only uses U.S. M2 is not a global liquidity chart.

## When the BTC-versus-liquidity correlation breaks

The relationship between BTC and global liquidity loosens or breaks under several conditions. Being aware of these is essential before using any liquidity chart as a market-timing tool.

**Idiosyncratic crypto catalysts.** The 2022 FTX collapse, the 2021 China mining ban, and the 2020 COVID flush all moved BTC independent of liquidity conditions. External shocks override the macro framework without warning. During these periods, BTC diverged significantly from what global liquidity alone would have predicted.

**ETF flow dominance.** Since the January 2024 U.S. spot ETF approval, sustained institutional inflows via regulated vehicles have created a demand channel that can override global liquidity signals. Strong ETF inflows paired with flat global liquidity can push BTC higher than the correlation would predict. For this reason, [Bitcoin ETF flow data](/etf-flows/bitcoin-etf/best-bitcoin-etf-flow-trackers-2026) is now a necessary companion to any global liquidity analysis.

**Positioning squeezes.** Large short squeezes and leverage unwinds can move BTC 10-20% in hours, independent of any macro condition. These are derivatives-market events, not macro events, and they create noise in the BTC-versus-liquidity chart that has no macro interpretation.

**Lag ambiguity.** Some analysts use a 3-month, some use a 6-month, and some use a 12-month lag between liquidity and BTC price. The choice of lag dramatically changes which direction the correlation appears to move. A chart that shows a tight correlation with a 6-month lag is not proof that the 6-month lag is the correct one; it may be the lag that was chosen specifically because it fit the data.

## What a responsible methodology disclosure looks like

Any published analysis using a BTC-versus-liquidity chart should disclose:

1. Which liquidity series is used (exact FRED series codes or named source)
2. Whether the series is adjusted for exchange rates and, if so, which rates
3. The timeframe of the chart
4. Whether a lag is applied, and how that lag was chosen
5. Whether the correlation broke during the observed period and why

Without these disclosures, a liquidity chart is a visual argument, not an analytical claim. MarketBit should hold all cited liquidity charts to this standard.

## The 7 best global liquidity charts for Bitcoin in 2026

### 1. TradingView custom global liquidity overlays

[TradingView](https://www.tradingview.com/) is the most flexible charting environment on this list for building BTC-versus-liquidity overlays. Analysts can combine BTC price with FRED macro series, community-built liquidity indicators, DXY, yields, and stablecoin supply proxies on one screen. No other tool on this list matches the overlay flexibility.

The quality of a TradingView liquidity chart depends entirely on the indicators used. A chart built from named FRED series with a documented methodology is publishable. A chart using an unnamed community script with no methodology disclosure is not. TradingView enables both, which means the platform value for serious analysis depends on the analyst discipline, not just the tool capability.

Bitcoin forums on Reddit have discussed TradingView global liquidity community scripts in macro analysis threads since at least 2021. The recurring observation is that the script quality varies significantly: some pull legitimate global M2 composites, others pull only U.S. M2 and label it global without clarification. Checking which series a script uses before publishing is a basic methodology step that the discussion threads consistently recommend.

**Best for:** analysts who want to build custom BTC-versus-liquidity overlays and are comfortable verifying indicator methodology.
**Main tradeoff:** flexibility without discipline produces unreliable charts. The tool does not enforce methodology quality; the analyst does. Whether TradingView is the right starting point depends on whether the workflow requires custom overlay control or a pre-built chart.

### 2. FRED-based liquidity builds

[FRED](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/) (Federal Reserve Bank of St. Louis Economic Data) is the most transparent free macro data source on this list. It provides named, versioned, documented time series for U.S. M2, central bank balance sheets, reserve balances, credit conditions, and hundreds of additional macro indicators. Every series is citable by name and code.

For Bitcoin liquidity analysis, the most commonly used FRED series are: `M2SL` (U.S. M2 Money Stock), `WALCL` (Federal Reserve total assets), and `RRPONTSYD` (overnight reverse repo facility, as a financial-conditions indicator). For a rough global composite, `JPNASSETS` and `ECBASSETS` can be added alongside U.S. data.

The chart built from FRED series is not the most visually polished on this list. FRED interface is designed for data access, not market narrative. But it is the most defensible chart for any published analysis because every line can be traced to a public, versioned, government-produced data series.

A macro analysis thread on a Bitcoin forum on Reddit that walked through a BTC-versus-M2 divergence in late 2023 linked directly to FRED series rather than to a social-media screenshot. The thread noted that using FRED data meant any reader could check the underlying numbers themselves, which several commenters did. That transparency standard is the benchmark for any credible liquidity analysis.

**Best for:** analysts and publications that need fully transparent, reproducible, citable liquidity data for published analysis.
**Main tradeoff:** FRED is a data library, not a charting tool. Building the BTC overlay requires combining FRED data with TradingView or another charting environment. The additional step is worth it for any published work.

### 3. MacroMicro

[MacroMicro](https://en.macromicro.me/) provides a packaged global macro dashboard that includes pre-built global M2 composite views, central bank balance-sheet comparisons, and Bitcoin correlation charts. The product is designed for readers who want clean macro outputs without building custom charts from FRED source series.

The MacroMicro global liquidity chart aggregates M2 data from the U.S., EU, China, Japan, and other major economies into a single composite, displayed alongside Bitcoin price. The methodology is more transparent than a typical social-media liquidity chart because MacroMicro names the components and describes the construction. It is less transparent than a full FRED build because the specific series and weighting are not user-adjustable.

MacroMicro appears in macro discussion threads on Bitcoin forums as a reference for readers who want a reliable pre-built global liquidity chart without constructing one from FRED themselves. The site accessibility relative to raw FRED data is consistently noted as its main practical advantage.

**Best for:** analysts who need a clean, reliable, pre-built global liquidity view with Bitcoin comparison, without custom chart construction.
**Main tradeoff:** methodology is less transparent and less adjustable than a FRED build. Weighting of country components is fixed. For published analysis, disclosing which MacroMicro series is used is necessary for credibility.

### 4. BGeometrics

[BGeometrics](https://bgeometrics.com/) provides purpose-built Bitcoin-versus-global-liquidity visualizations. The product is designed specifically for analysts and researchers who want a dedicated BTC/liquidity correlation view rather than a general macro dashboard.

BGeometrics is most useful for readers who want a pre-built liquidity chart with a Bitcoin lens already applied, without the general-purpose overhead of MacroMicro or FRED. The focus is tighter, which makes it faster to use for the specific question of BTC versus macro liquidity.

**Best for:** dedicated BTC-versus-liquidity correlation views for readers who want a tool built specifically for this use case.
**Main tradeoff:** narrower scope than MacroMicro and less data transparency than FRED. Useful as a first-reference chart; requires supplementing with source data for published analysis. Whether BGeometrics fits the workflow depends on whether a pre-built BTC correlation view is the end goal or a starting point.

### 5. CrossBorder Capital

[CrossBorder Capital](https://crossbordercapital.com/) provides a formal macro-liquidity framework that uses central bank balance sheets, credit conditions, and capital flow data rather than M2 alone. The firm liquidity index is a proprietary composite that goes deeper than any publicly accessible dataset.

The institutional depth is CrossBorder Capital main advantage. Its liquidity framing is more complete than a simple M2 overlay because it captures credit conditions and cross-border capital flows that M2 misses. The limitation for most readers is access: the full framework is institutional-priced and not freely accessible.

CrossBorder Capital public commentary and data summaries are available and widely cited in institutional macro research. For MarketBit analysis, the firm published framing is citable as a reference even without full data access.

**Best for:** institutional macro readers who need a formal liquidity framework beyond M2 aggregation.
**Main tradeoff:** institutional pricing limits access. Most practical for MarketBit as a citation source for macro-liquidity framing rather than a direct data tool. Whether the subscription is justified depends on whether institutional-grade liquidity framing is core to the editorial workflow.

### 6. Artemis stablecoin-supply dashboards

[Artemis](https://www.artemisanalytics.com/) provides cross-chain stablecoin supply data that functions as a crypto-native liquidity proxy. Growing stablecoin supply means dry powder is accumulating inside the crypto ecosystem. Contracting supply means capital is rotating out.

Stablecoin supply is not a substitute for global macro liquidity. It measures a different phenomenon: liquidity available within crypto markets rather than in the broader financial system. But the two signals often move together during risk-on and risk-off cycles, and divergences between them are informative. A period where global macro liquidity is flat but stablecoin supply is growing signals that crypto-native capital is accumulating independently of macro conditions.

For [stablecoin supply analysis](/liquidity/stablecoins/best-stablecoin-dashboards-2026), Artemis provides cleaner cross-chain historical comparison than most platforms on this list.

**Best for:** analysts who want a crypto-native liquidity proxy to pair alongside traditional global liquidity charts.
**Main tradeoff:** stablecoin supply is a parallel signal, not a replacement for global macro liquidity data. Whether Artemis belongs in the stack depends on whether the analysis covers crypto-native liquidity dynamics or only traditional macro liquidity.

### 7. Dune stablecoin and flow dashboards

[Dune](https://dune.com/) enables custom on-chain stablecoin and capital-flow dashboards built from raw blockchain data. An analyst can build a view of stablecoin inflows to centralized exchanges, stablecoin minting events, and cross-chain stablecoin movements using public SQL queries.

The Dune approach to crypto liquidity is the most reproducible on this list. Because every dashboard is built from named, publicly accessible on-chain queries, any reader can verify the methodology and rebuild the chart themselves. That reproducibility standard matches what FRED provides for traditional macro data.

Dune community dashboards tracking stablecoin flows have been cited in DeFi forum discussions on Reddit when traders wanted to understand whether USDT or USDC inflows to exchanges were signaling buying pressure. The discussion threads noted that Dune public query model meant the methodology was verifiable, which influenced how much weight readers gave to the data.

**Best for:** analysts who need reproducible, custom on-chain crypto-liquidity views from named, verifiable data sources.
**Main tradeoff:** requires SQL familiarity to build custom views. For readers who want a pre-built dashboard, searching Dune for existing stablecoin-flow dashboards before building is usually the faster starting point.

## Frequently asked questions

### Does Bitcoin always follow global liquidity?

No. Bitcoin often responds to changes in global liquidity conditions, but the relationship is not constant, not perfectly timed, and breaks during idiosyncratic crypto events, ETF flow dominance, and positioning squeezes. Treating it as a mechanical relationship produces bad analysis.

### What is the best free global liquidity chart for Bitcoin?

FRED is the most transparent and fully free source for the underlying macro data. MacroMicro provides a pre-built global liquidity view that is partially free. TradingView with community indicators is free and flexible but requires verifying indicator methodology before use.

### What is global M2 and why does it matter for Bitcoin?

Global M2 is an approximation of the total money supply across major economies, usually combining U.S., EU, Japan, and China M2 converted to USD. It is used as a proxy for available liquidity in the global financial system. When M2 expands, more capital is available to flow into risk assets including BTC. The relationship is real but loose and subject to many exceptions.

### How do I build a Bitcoin-versus-liquidity chart on TradingView?

Use FRED series M2SL for U.S. M2 and ECBASSETS and JPNASSETS for ECB and BOJ balance sheets as overlays on a BTCUSD weekly chart. Normalize each series to percentage change from a common start date. For a faster start, search TradingView indicator library for community global liquidity scripts, then verify which series they use before publishing any analysis based on them.

### What should I pair a liquidity chart with?

[Bitcoin ETF flows](/etf-flows/bitcoin-etf/best-bitcoin-etf-flow-trackers-2026), [stablecoin supply growth](/liquidity/stablecoins/best-stablecoin-dashboards-2026), and derivatives positioning are the most informative companions. A liquidity chart without these companion signals produces an incomplete picture of why BTC is moving in a given period.

### Why does BTC sometimes move before global liquidity changes?

Bitcoin moves in advance of confirmed liquidity readings for several reasons: markets price in expected liquidity changes before they are confirmed in official data, ETF flows or crypto-native stablecoin inflows can precede broader macro liquidity expansion, and positioning dynamics can produce price moves that have nothing to do with macro conditions at all. The leading or lagging relationship between BTC and liquidity changes from cycle to cycle.
